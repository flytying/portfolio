## Project Summary

BillTurtle was a startup concept I initiated in 2019 to address a common but frustrating problem: missed bill payments caused by fragmented reminders, delayed paper statements, and inconvenient payment habits.

The idea was to create a mobile experience that would help people track their bills in one place, understand which payments needed attention, and reduce the friction of managing multiple billers manually. I led the product and UI/UX design work for the concept, from early research and user flows through wireframes, MVP definition, and high-fidelity mockups.

## The Problem

At the time, bill payment for many users was still highly fragmented.

People often depended on paper bills, manual reminders, or memory to keep track of due dates. Delays in bill delivery could lead to overdue payments, and managing several billers at once created unnecessary effort. Even when people intended to stay on top of their bills, the overall process was often reactive instead of organized.

The problem was not simply "how to pay a bill online." It was broader:

- Users did not always have one clear place to monitor all billers
- Reminders and bill status were inconsistent or delayed
- The experience of tracking what was due, paid, or overdue was often manual
- Managing multiple recurring bills created avoidable cognitive load
- Existing payment behavior still involved too much friction

This made the opportunity less about payments alone and more about bill visibility, reminders, and control.

## Product Goal

The goal of BillTurtle was to create a mobile product that could help users stay ahead of their bills rather than react to them late.

The product was designed to:

- Centralize bill tracking across multiple billers
- Make due, paid, and overdue states easier to understand
- Reduce missed payments through reminders and notifications
- Create a simpler experience for managing recurring household bills
- Establish the foundation for a more convenient digital billing workflow

## Why This Problem Mattered

This idea started from a personal pain point, but the opportunity was larger than my own experience.

I had experienced the inconvenience of receiving paper bills late and dealing with the hassle of paying multiple bills through disconnected processes. That led me to explore whether this was an isolated frustration or a broader user problem worth solving.

What became clear was that the friction was not just about payments. It was about a lack of visibility and coordination around recurring obligations. That insight shaped the product direction from the beginning.

## My Role

I led the product direction and UI/UX design for BillTurtle. My work included:

- Identifying and framing the product opportunity
- Researching existing bill payment behaviors and market conditions
- Mapping user flows and defining the MVP structure
- Creating wireframes and interface concepts
- Refining the product through feedback and iteration
- Developing high-fidelity mockups for the mobile app
- Helping shape the product for accelerator presentation and early validation

## Research and Preparation

Before designing the product, I spent several months researching the bills payment landscape.

This included looking at:

- How existing billers handled payment and communication
- How users currently kept track of due dates and statuses
- The operational differences between billers
- Where friction existed in the end-to-end experience
- How ready the market was for a more digital bill management product

At the time, digital bill management and online bill payment were not yet as normalized as they are today. That created an interesting opportunity: there was room for a product that could simplify the experience before digital-first habits became standard.

The goal of this phase was not just to gather information. It was to determine whether there was a real behavior gap that a focused product could solve.

## Defining the MVP

Once the opportunity became clearer, I moved into early product definition.

I created the initial sitemaps and user flows to understand what the first usable version of BillTurtle should include. Rather than trying to build a broad financial app, I focused on a narrower use case:

- Adding and managing billers
- Seeing which bills required attention
- Understanding payment status at a glance
- Receiving billing reminders and updates
- Creating a lightweight overview of personal bill activity

This helped keep the product grounded in a clear user job: help me avoid missing bills and make my billing obligations easier to manage.

## Wireframes and Early Concept Development

The wireframes were shaped around everyday bill management behavior rather than around feature quantity.

I used the early concepts to test how the product should organize information such as:

- Upcoming bills
- Paid and overdue bills
- Individual biller details
- Reminders and notifications
- Basic bill status tracking

I also gathered feedback from people around me, including family and friends, to understand how they currently handled their bills and where they felt the most friction. That input helped make the early concepts more practical and less assumption-driven.

![Wireframes exploring product structure and user flows](/projects/billturtle/wireframes.webp)

## Design Direction

The product direction centered on clarity, reassurance, and low-friction management.

I designed the experience around three key principles:

**1. Make bill status easy to understand**
Users should be able to quickly tell what is paid, what is due soon, and what needs urgent attention.

**2. Reduce mental load**
The product needed to act as an external memory system for recurring bills, so users would not have to rely on paper statements or memory.

**3. Keep the experience lightweight**
Because bill management is a recurring utility task, the app needed to feel simple and supportive rather than overloaded with complexity.

This is why the concepts leaned toward straightforward summaries, biller-based organization, and reminders that surfaced the right information at the right time.

## Accelerator and Validation

As the product became more defined, I joined a local startup accelerator, Ideaspace Foundation, and formed a small team with two developers to support the MVP direction.

This stage was important because it pushed the project beyond internal thinking and into a more critical evaluation environment. Through the accelerator process, we received feedback that helped sharpen the product concept, challenge assumptions, and improve the wireframes.

The value of this stage was not only mentorship. It was validation pressure. It forced the product to become clearer, more focused, and more realistic.

## High-Fidelity Concept

The final high-fidelity mockups translated the MVP into a more polished mobile product experience.

The UI emphasized:

- An overview of bills needing attention
- Clear separation of paid, due, and overdue states
- Easy access to biller-related information
- A friendly and approachable visual tone for an otherwise utility-heavy task
- A simple onboarding direction for first-time users

The final concept was designed to make bill management feel less stressful and more organized, while keeping the product approachable for everyday users.

## Early Traction and Outcome

Although BillTurtle did not progress to full launch, the project reached a meaningful stage.

We completed the high-fidelity concept work, pushed the idea through accelerator feedback, and secured early interest from a local gym as a potential first client. We were also discussing implementation of both the mobile app and dashboard experience.

The project ultimately stopped when COVID-19 disrupted business operations and made it difficult to continue moving the startup forward. The team made the difficult decision to part ways.

Even so, the project was valuable because it moved beyond a speculative design exercise. It reached a stage of real product shaping, external feedback, and early market signal.

## Why This Project Matters in My Portfolio

BillTurtle reflects how I think when I am not just designing screens, but helping shape a product from the ground up.

It shows my ability to:

- Identify product opportunities from real user pain
- Research a market before moving into solutions
- Define an MVP around core user behavior
- Translate early strategy into flows and interfaces
- Refine a concept through feedback and validation
- Design with both usability and product viability in mind

## Key Learnings

This project taught me several lessons that still shape how I approach product design.

**A strong product starts with a behavior problem, not just an interface opportunity**
What made BillTurtle interesting was not the idea of "a payment app," but the specific behavior gap around tracking and managing recurring bills.

**MVPs become stronger when they stay close to one clear job**
The product improved once it focused less on broad fintech ambition and more on one practical user need: helping people stay on top of bills.

**Feedback is most useful when it challenges the concept, not just the screens**
The accelerator experience helped refine not only the UI, but the product thinking behind it.

**A project can still be valuable even if it does not launch**
BillTurtle remains an important case study because it demonstrates early-stage product thinking, validation work, and design leadership under startup constraints.
